What's Actually Happening, The Numbers That Should Stop You Mid-Scroll

Let me give you two numbers.

Publicis Groupe: +5.6% organic growth. €14.5 billion in revenue.

WPP: -8.1% revenue decline. Market cap destroyed, from approximately £25 billion down to roughly £3 billion.

These are two of the world's largest advertising holding companies. They compete in the same markets. They serve the same types of clients, global FMCG brands, automotive companies, consumer tech, financial services. They fight for the same talent. They face the same pressures.

The difference is this: Publicis started investing in AI infrastructure nearly a decade ago.

They built Marcel, their internal AI platform that connects their entire global workforce, long before it was fashionable to have "AI" anywhere near your investor day presentation. They restructured, retrained, and rewired the entire organization around AI as a foundational capability, not a feature.

WPP is restructuring now. Reactively. In crisis mode.

The difference in outcomes isn't subtle. It's existential.

And it's not just advertising.

JPMorgan has told managers to stop hiring across multiple business units. A headcount reduction of more than 10% is underway. Goldman Sachs is actively limiting its growth in areas where AI is replacing junior analyst work. Across Wall Street, analysts estimate that more than 200,000 jobs are at risk.

Amazon cut 30,000 corporate roles. Not warehouse workers. Corporate roles. Retail workforce cuts across the industry are up 123% year over year.

In the tech sector, Wisetech Global cut 30% of its staff. Their CEO said something I keep coming back to:

"The era of writing code is over."

More than 141,000 technology industry jobs have been eliminated in 2025 alone.

This is a structural realignment, happening across industries, in real time.

The Analogy That Puts This in Perspective

Let me take you back to 2005.

Physical retailers knew e-commerce was coming. Amazon existed. The data was there. The trend was obvious to anyone who wanted to see it.

Most retailers said some version of the same thing: "We'll figure out online. We've got time. Our customers still come to the store. Our brand is strong. We'll adapt when we need to."

They didn't deny the trend. They just believed they had more runway than they did.

By 2015, Borders was gone. Circuit City was gone. Blockbuster was already a cultural punchline. Hundreds of retailers were closing stores faster than they could renegotiate their leases.

The companies that went under weren't run by stupid people. They were run by smart people who kept finding reasons to delay the hard call.

The compression from "emerging trend" to "industry-defining force" took about a decade. AI is compressing that same arc, from decade to years. Maybe less.
