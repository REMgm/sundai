The machine just got an effort dial

Claude shipped with a literal effort control this week. Ask it to reformat a list and it doesn't break a sweat -- handles it on low, one hand on the wheel, sunglasses on. Ask it to redesign a system that touches money or security and it cracks its knuckles, pours a coffee, and goes deep. It reads the stakes and spends its energy where they actually live.

A machine. Triaging its own effort. Like a tiny digital monk who has decided your formatting request is beneath enlightenment.

I stared at that dial for a long time. Then I laughed -- the slightly unhinged laugh of a man realizing the software just out-managed him -- because it hit me: the machine got better at allocating effort than most of the leaders I work with. And it did it on a Tuesday, by accident, as a feature update.

Here's the uncomfortable part

You already have an effort dial. You've had one your whole life. It sits right behind your forehead. And if you're like most brilliant, exhausted, high-output people I know, you run it exactly backwards.

You crank it to max to wordsmith a Slack message nobody will remember by lunch. You agonize over the font on a deck. You rewrite an email four times. Full horsepower, engine redlined -- on a task that needed low and a send button.

Then the genuinely heavy decision arrives. The hire. The strategy bet. The "do we kill this product or double down" call. And by the time it lands, you're running on fumes. You make a quarter-defining choice on low effort because you spent your max on the inbox.

The machine doesn't make that mistake. That's the whole upgrade. It looks at a task, asks whether it actually deserves the deepest thinking, and most of the time, honestly, the answer is no. So it conserves. It saves the deep reasoning for the fraction of work where depth changes the outcome.

We spent a decade teaching AI to think harder. The breakthrough was teaching it to think less.

We call that wisdom when a person does it. We just shipped it as a feature.

The thing nobody tells you about effort

Effort isn't a virtue. We've been raised to believe it is -- that the harder you grind, the better the human you are. So we wear maximum-effort-on-everything like a badge.

Effort isn't a medal. It's a budget.

You get a finite amount each day. Spending it is a choice whether you admit it or not. And spreading it evenly across everything isn't discipline -- it's just refusing to decide what matters.

Here's the cruel part. The reason brilliant, tired people burn out isn't the volume of work. I've watched people carry enormous volumes and stay lit up like a Christmas tree. Burnout is more specific than that.

Burnout is rarely too much work. It's max effort on the wrong things, on repeat.

Your Sunday assignment

Tomorrow, before you touch a single task, run it through three questions. Steal them. They're free.

Three questions, every task

1. Will this matter in 90 days? If no, it's a low task. Do it fast, do it good-enough, move on. "Good enough" on a 90-day-irrelevant task isn't lowering your standards. It's having them.

2. Does this complete, or does this compound? A task that completes is done forever -- file the report, answer the message, close the ticket. Low. A task that compounds -- the relationship, the system, the skill, the trust -- pays you back every day after. That's where your max goes. Every time.

3. Could a rested version of me do this in half the time? If yes, the problem was never the task. It was the tank. Refuel, and stop pretending that grinding on empty is noble. It's just slow.

The flip

We've spent years afraid the machines would out-think us. Funny how it actually played out. They never beat us at thinking harder -- that was never close, they win that one on raw volume. The day they got interesting is the day they learned to think less, on purpose, in the right places.

Which means the edge left for us -- the genuinely human edge -- isn't grinding. It's judgment. Knowing which fires to walk past. Knowing when "fine" is the strategically correct answer. Knowing where the vital fraction lives and pouring everything into it while everyone else exhausts themselves at maximum effort on things that don't move.

The machine got an effort dial this week. You've had yours the whole time. This is the Sunday you finally turn it the right way.
