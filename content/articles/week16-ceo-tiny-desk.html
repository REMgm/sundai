Both, independently, pulled up a chair at the smallest desk they could find and asked a junior team member to teach them how to use AI. What happened next was nearly identical -- and it changed how I think about executive leadership in the age of agents.

The Deep Dive: Why the Corner Office Is the Worst Place to Understand AI

The Simple Version

You cannot understand what AI can do for your organization by reading reports, attending briefings, or watching demos. You have to use it. And you have to use it badly, publicly, and with help from the people who work for you. The moment you become a student, you become approachable. And the moment you become approachable, your team's best ideas start surfacing.

The Context

Here's what happens when an executive treats AI as a strategic initiative they delegate: they get updates. The updates are filtered. Filtered updates contain only what the person giving the update thinks the executive wants to hear. The real friction, the real opportunities, the real breakthroughs -- they never make it up the chain.

Here's what happens when that same executive spends two hours a week actually using AI tools: they develop opinions. Those opinions are grounded in reality, not vendor presentations. They start asking questions that only someone who has tried the work would know to ask. And -- this is the part that surprises everyone -- they start having conversations with their team that sound completely different.

Let me tell you how I know.

Executive A -- the CMO. She runs marketing for a consumer brand you'd recognize. When we started coaching together, her AI strategy was a twelve-page deck her team had prepared. It was polished. It was comprehensive. It was entirely theoretical. She had never opened Claude, never written a prompt, never felt the friction of getting AI to do something useful. I told her to block ninety minutes every Tuesday. Sit in the open workspace -- not her office -- and work through real tasks with AI. She resisted for two weeks. Then she did it.

The first session was, in her words, "humiliating." She asked questions her team had answered months ago. She struggled with prompts that her junior content strategist could write in her sleep. She texted me: "I feel like a fraud."

Then week three happened. She was working through a prompt chain, trying to get Claude to analyze a campaign brief and generate strategic recommendations. One of her senior strategists -- someone she had worked with for three years but had never had an informal conversation with -- walked past, saw what she was doing, and stopped. "You know," she said, "if you reframe that first prompt to ask for the constraints instead of the output, you'll get something way more useful." She sat down. They spent forty-five minutes iterating together. By the end, they had a prompt sequence that the strategist took back to her team and that the CMO now uses in every planning session.

That forty-five-minute conversation produced more actionable insight than any quarterly strategy review she had attended in the previous year. Not because she's brilliant. Because she was sitting at the tiny desk, not the corner office. Because she was a peer, not a boss. Because her strategist felt comfortable correcting her.

Executive B -- the COO. His story is different but the pattern is identical. He runs operations at a SaaS company scaling fast. His AI exposure had been entirely through vendor demos and board presentations. I gave him the same assignment: ninety minutes, open workspace, real work.

He chose a different approach. Instead of working alone, he asked a 26-year-old data scientist on his team to teach him how to chain prompts. Sat at her desk. Used her setup. Let her drive. By the second session, he was asking questions she'd never heard from leadership -- not "what's the ROI?" but "why does this step take three prompts when it feels like it should take one?" By the third session, she was bringing him problems she'd been sitting on for months, problems she never would have escalated formally because they weren't "strategic enough." Two of those problems turned into the most impactful process improvements his operations team shipped that quarter.

The Analogy

Think about a CEO who has never used their company's product. They know the revenue numbers, the market share, the competitive landscape. They can present the strategy deck flawlessly. But ask them what a real customer experiences in minute three of using the product and they draw a blank.

Every executive understands that's a liability. That's why great CEOs -- Bezos, Cook, the ones who build companies that last -- use their own products religiously. Not because they need to place orders or book rides. Because understanding the product at the level of lived experience changes every strategic decision they make.

AI is no different. If your only experience with AI is vendor demos and strategy decks, you are making decisions about a product you have never actually used. You are strategically planning for a capability you don't personally understand. And your team knows it.

The moment you start using the tools your team uses, something shifts. You stop asking "what can AI do for us?" and start asking "why does this workflow take three steps when it could take one?" You stop evaluating AI initiatives and start co-creating them. You stop being the person who approves the budget and start being the person who spots the opportunity.

What I Observed as Their Coach

First, the quality of AI conversations skyrockets. Before the CMO started practicing, her team would present AI initiatives with elaborate business cases and ROI projections. They were selling her. Now, they bring half-formed ideas, rough prototypes, and experimental prompts. They're collaborating with her. The difference is profound: selling produces approved projects. Collaboration produces better ideas.

Second, ideas start flowing that would never have surfaced formally. The COO's data scientist showed him a prompt sequence that automated operational monitoring. A junior developer walked him through an agent he'd built to triage support tickets. These weren't strategic initiatives. They were experiments. Third -- and both executives reported this independently -- their own strategic thinking changed. When you actually use AI, you develop an intuition for what's easy, what's hard, and what's about to become possible. Both started seeing opportunities they would have missed before. Both started asking their teams different questions. Both started making better decisions about where to invest, where to experiment, and where to wait. Not because I taught them that. Because the tiny desk taught them that.

Your One Action This Week

This week, become a student. Not metaphorically. Actually. Here's how:

1. Block ninety minutes. Put it on your calendar. Protect it like you would a board meeting. Tuesday morning worked for me because it's early enough that I haven't been pulled into executive mode yet. Find your time.

2. Sit where your team sits. Not your office. The open workspace. The communal area. Somewhere people walk past and can see what you're doing. The visibility matters. It signals that this is important enough for your time and public enough for your ego.

3. Pick one tool and actually use it. Not a demo. Not a tutorial video. Real work. Analyze a brief. Draft an email chain. Build a spreadsheet. Do something you would normally do manually, and do it with AI. You'll be slower at first. That's the point.

4. Ask for help. Publicly. This is the hardest part and the most important. Ask the junior person on your team to show you how they use the tool. Ask the developer to walk you through their workflow. Let them see you struggle. Let them teach you. The moment you ask for help, you give them permission to be experts -- and you give yourself access to their expertise.

5. Have one real conversation. Not a status update. Not a strategic review. A conversation. Ask what they're experimenting with. Ask what frustrated them this week. Ask what they would build if they had two weeks and no meetings. When you speak as a practitioner, not a boss, the answers will surprise you.

The Prediction

I've now given this assignment to over a dozen executives. The pattern is consistent enough that I'll make a prediction:

Within a month of starting this practice, you will have at least one conversation that produces more value than a quarter of your formal meetings. Within three months, your team will bring you ideas they never would have surfaced to an executive. Within six months, you will understand your organization's AI opportunities better than any consultant ever could.

The executives who treat AI as something to delegate will spend 2026 reading reports about opportunities their teams already found. The executives who become practitioners will find those opportunities themselves -- and their teams will bring them even more.

The CMO told me last week that her Tuesday practice is now the most productive ninety minutes of her week. The COO said something even more telling: "I used to think my job was to set the AI strategy. Now I realize my job is to be good enough at AI that my team isn't afraid to show me what they've actually built."

The corner office has a great view. But the tiny desk is where the good ideas live.
