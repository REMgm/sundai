The Numbers Behind the Urgency

JPMorgan has 250,000 employees registered on their internal AI platform. 60% of them use it daily. This is not a small experiment at the innovation lab. This is a quarter million people incorporating AI into their normal work, every day.

Companies with formal AI strategies succeed at implementation 80% of the time. Companies without them succeed 37% of the time. That 43-percentage-point gap is one of the largest performance differentials in any major technology adoption study I've seen.

48% of employees say that formal training would meaningfully increase their AI usage. Nearly half your team is ready to move. They are waiting for structure, permission, and a starting point.

You have the power to give them all three. This week.

The Day-by-Day Playbook

Here is your week. Block the time now. Don't treat this as "if I get to it." Schedule it.

Monday: Use AI yourself. One hour. A real task.

This is the most important day of the week because it's the one most leaders skip.

Set aside one hour. Close Outlook. Put your phone down. Pick one real task that's sitting in your inbox, an email you've been avoiding, a document you need to synthesize, a brief you need to write.

Open an AI tool, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, it doesn't matter which one, and use it to actually do the task.

That forty-five minutes of firsthand experience will make you a better leader of this transformation than any briefing document ever written.

Tuesday: Survey your team. Two questions.

Send a note to your direct reports with exactly two questions:

"What AI tools are you currently using, for anything, personal or professional?"

"What are the three most repetitive tasks in your current role?"

Your team is already using AI. Widely, quietly, and often without telling you. And the repetitive tasks they describe are almost certainly your highest-ROI AI opportunities.

Wednesday: Pick three opportunities.

Take what you learned on Tuesday and apply one filter.

You're looking for opportunities that meet three criteria simultaneously: high impact on real work, low risk if something goes wrong, and measurable within 90 days.

Three is plenty. Three is actually ambitious if you execute on them properly. The temptation will be to pick twelve. Resist it.

Constraint is the friend of execution.

Thursday: Name your AI champions.

Every company has people who others turn to when something new comes along. They're not always the most technically sophisticated people. They're the most connected people, the ones whose enthusiasm is contagious, whose credibility is trusted.

Think about who those people are. Name them. Three to five people.

You're not creating a committee. You're identifying the people through whom change actually travels.

Friday: Tell everyone.

Not in a vague, aspirational, "exciting AI journey ahead" way.

Specifically.

This is what we're going to work on. (The three opportunities from Wednesday, named specifically.)

This is why it matters. (Connect it to your team's work and your customers' experience.)

This is the goal we're holding ourselves to in the next 90 days. (A concrete, measurable target.)

Transparency is not weakness here. It's the mechanism. When people know specifically what you're aiming at, they can contribute to it.

Monks Success Story

When Monks committed to AI as a core capability, it didn't start with a software selection.

It started with a structural decision.

We collapsed from a holding company with multiple brands into a single, unified organization. We made Wesley ter Haar, our co-founder, both Chief AI Officer and Chief Revenue Officer simultaneously. That dual role was deliberate. It said: AI is not a technology function living in IT. It is a revenue function.

We built Monks Foundry, a team of 150 NVIDIA-certified engineers embedded directly into our production infrastructure.

We assembled a 50-person Agentic Advisory team dedicated entirely to helping clients build AI-native operating models.

None of that happened in the first week. But the first week was a decision. Not a budget. Not a vendor. Not a consultant engagement.

A decision.

"The biggest myth about AI transformation is that you need to have it all figured out before you start. You don't. You just need to start."
