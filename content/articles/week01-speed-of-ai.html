The Deep Dive: Why the Pace Itself Is the Point

The Simple Version

AI is not just getting better. It's getting better faster than anything we've seen in modern business history. And the speed of change, not any individual tool or model, is now the thing you need to build your strategy around.

The Context

Let me give you a number that stopped me in my tracks the first time I saw it.

In 2020, there were 11 notable AI models released globally. That includes everything from the models powering research labs to the ones beginning to show up in enterprise software. Eleven. You could track them. You could evaluate them. You had time.

In 2024, that number was 81. And in 2025, we're tracking meaningful new model releases every single week, often multiple per week, from multiple labs, across multiple countries.

That's not a trend line. That's a new operating environment.

And critically, it's not just volume, it's capability at collapsing cost. The price of running AI inference, the cost of actually using these models to process tasks, has dropped dramatically. Work that cost $100 to process in 2023 now costs $10 or less. In many categories, the cost is approaching fractions of a cent per task. The economic floor keeps dropping, and it drops fast.

Here's the moment that made this viscerally real for global markets.

In January 2025, a Chinese AI lab called DeepSeek released a model that matched, and in some benchmarks beat, the leading American AI systems, including OpenAI's flagship GPT-4 class models. The extraordinary thing wasn't the performance. It was the cost. DeepSeek reportedly trained this model for approximately $6 million. For context, comparable American models had cost upwards of $100 million to develop.

The reaction was immediate and historic. Nvidia's stock dropped $589 billion in a single trading day, the largest single-day market capitalization loss for any company in history. That number didn't represent panic. It represented an entire industry repricing its assumptions about what AI costs, who can build it, and how wide any competitive moat actually is.

If AI capability at that level can be built for $6 million, the cost structure of the entire industry shifts overnight. The barrier to entry drops. The number of players increases. The pace accelerates further.

This is what I mean when I say the pace itself is the point. We're not living through a steady improvement curve. We're living through a compounding acceleration where each breakthrough reduces the cost and time required for the next one.

The leaders I talk with most often frame their approach as "keeping an eye on AI." I understand what they mean, they're not ignoring it, they're monitoring it. But keeping an eye on AI in 2025 is structurally similar to keeping an eye on the internet in 2001. The window for passive observation is closing. The window for building is wide open, but not indefinitely.

Companies that made meaningful commitments in 2023 and 2024 now have 18 to 24 months of real-world learning embedded in their systems. That gap compounds. And unlike capital, organizational learning isn't something you can acquire quickly by spending money.

The pace is the point. Let's talk about what that means for how you think about it.

The Analogy

Think about internet speed, something every business leader already understands intuitively.

In 2020, AI was dial-up. You could do basic things. It was slow, unreliable, and you built your business strategy around its limitations. Yes, it existed. Yes, some people were using it. But the use cases were narrow, the results were inconsistent, and most executives were right to treat it as a novelty.

By 2023, we had broadband. Meaningfully faster, more reliable, genuinely useful for real business tasks. But still with constraints. You could do quite a bit, but you were still running into walls regularly.

In 2025, we have fiber optic AI. The infrastructure constraint is effectively gone. The question is no longer "is this good enough to be useful?" It's "what are we going to build now that the infrastructure supports it?"

Here's the thing about internet speed that's easy to forget: you couldn't have run Netflix on dial-up. It wasn't a Netflix problem, it was an infrastructure problem. The business model was sound. The customer desire was there. The infrastructure just wasn't ready.

That infrastructure constraint is now solved for AI. Which means the question has shifted entirely to: what business model do you want to build on top of it?

The companies asking that question now, and building the answers, are the ones that will look like Netflix in 2010. Everyone else is still deciding whether streaming is worth paying attention to.

Monks Success Story

I've seen this play out firsthand inside Monks, and I want to share it because it illustrates what "moving fast" actually looks like in practice, not as an abstract principle, but as a sequence of real decisions.

In January 2024, at CES in Las Vegas, we unveiled a concept: an AI-native marketing platform that would cover the full lifecycle of content and campaigns, from strategy and creative ideation through production, deployment, and performance optimization. We called it Monks.Flow.

At that point, it was a vision backed by early infrastructure. The models were advancing fast. The use cases were becoming clearer. We made a bet that the technology would get to where we needed it to be, and we started building toward that future state rather than waiting for it to arrive.

Eighteen months later, at Cannes Lions 2025, we presented Monks.Flow as a fully operational "agent marketing factory." Not a prototype. Not a proof of concept. A system running at scale for some of the most demanding brands in the world.

In those same 18 months, Monks was named Adweek's first-ever AI Agency of the Year. Not because we had the most sophisticated slides about AI. Because we had the most sophisticated deployment of it.

That trajectory, from concept at CES to operational platform at Cannes, in 18 months, was only possible because we started building on moving ground. The models changed. The capabilities shifted. New tools emerged that we hadn't anticipated. We had to rebuild parts of the architecture multiple times.

But because we were already operating, we could adapt. We had real-world feedback loops. We knew what was working and what wasn't. We were learning at a pace that only comes from doing, not watching.

The pace of AI means the advantage goes to organizations that accept the discomfort of building before everything is certain. That's uncomfortable. But it's also how the gap gets created, and maintained.

"You don't need to understand every AI model. You need to understand that the tools your competitors will use in six months are already available today, and they're getting better every week."

Your One Action This Week

This week, pick one real work task, a report you write, a brief you create, a presentation you build, a summary you pull together, and do it using ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini instead of starting from scratch.

Not a toy task. Not a test prompt. A real deliverable that you would normally produce yourself.

Don't try to automate your entire workflow. Don't design an AI strategy. Don't spend two hours figuring out the perfect prompt. Just use the tool for one real thing and notice what happens.

The goal isn't efficiency gains. The goal is calibration. You need a current, first-hand understanding of where AI actually is in its capabilities, not where it was the last time you tried it (probably 12–18 months ago), and not where a vendor told you it is.

The executives I know who are making the best AI decisions right now share one characteristic: they use AI tools regularly themselves. Not because they're technical, most of them aren't. Because they're curious, and because they understand that personal experience with the tools gives them a judgment about what's real versus what's hype that no briefing can replicate.

Start there. One task. This week.
