The Context

Here's what's changed in the last two years that makes this moment different from any previous point in AI's history.

Cost. What used to require millions of dollars in infrastructure now costs thousands, sometimes hundreds. Production-ready AI capabilities are accessible to mid-sized organizations.

Accessibility. No-code and low-code AI tools have matured dramatically. You don't need a PhD in machine learning to deploy AI in your marketing, operations, finance, or HR functions.

Infrastructure. Cloud AI platforms are now enterprise-ready with the compliance, security, and reliability that regulated industries require.

Talent. A generation of AI-native professionals is entering the workforce. The question is whether your organization is a place they want to bring those skills.

The World Economic Forum projects 170 million new jobs created by 2030 as a result of AI, against 92 million displaced. A net gain of 78 million jobs. AI creates more than it destroys.

The Historical Pattern

We have been here before. Three times in my professional lifetime.

The PC. In the early 1980s, the conventional wisdom was that computers would automate white-collar jobs out of existence. Instead, they created the entire modern knowledge economy.

The Internet. In the late 1990s, people said it was a bubble, it was overhyped, it would destroy more than it built. It created industries that didn't exist before: e-commerce, digital media, SaaS, social platforms, the gig economy.

Mobile. In 2008, serious analysts said smartphones would be a niche product. A decade later, mobile-first was the only strategy that mattered.

Every major technology wave looked like a threat from the outside and an opportunity from the inside.

The ones who engaged early didn't always win. But the ones who waited consistently fell further behind.
