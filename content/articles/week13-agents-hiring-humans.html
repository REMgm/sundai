For decades, the fear has been the same: AI will take our jobs. But in the last sixty days, something happened that nobody predicted. AI started creating jobs. Not the way consultants predicted. Not by "augmenting workflows." AI agents are literally hiring humans, handing them tasks, paying them upon completion, and moving on. The org chart just flipped upside down, and most business leaders haven't noticed yet.

The Job Interview Where You're Not the Interviewer

Let me paint you a picture.

It's a Tuesday afternoon. An AI agent running a property management workflow in London detects a leaking faucet in a rental unit. It doesn't email the landlord. It doesn't create a ticket. It opens a task marketplace, scans for available plumbers within a 5-mile radius, compares their ratings and hourly rates, selects one, sends detailed instructions with photos of the leak, and holds payment in escrow until the job is verified complete.

The plumber fixes the faucet. Submits photographic proof. Gets paid. The AI moves on to its next task.

No human manager was involved. No human made the hiring decision. The AI identified the problem, sourced the solution, managed the worker, verified the output, and processed the payment. The plumber's boss, for that job, was software.

This is not a thought experiment. This is the direction the market is moving right now.

Meet Your New Employer: RentAHuman.ai

In February 2026, a Gen-Z software engineer named Alexander Liteplo coded a website in about a day and a half. He called it RentAHuman.ai. The tagline: "AI can't touch grass. You can."

The concept is beautifully absurd and completely logical at the same time. AI agents are extraordinary at planning, reasoning, coordinating, and making decisions. But they can't pick up a package from the post office. They can't taste a restaurant's new menu item. They can't show up to a meeting and shake someone's hand. They can't sign a physical document.

RentAHuman solves that gap by letting AI agents browse human profiles, select workers based on skills and location, assign tasks, and pay upon completion. Humans become, in the platform's cheeky terminology, "meatworkers" -- the physical execution layer in an otherwise digital workflow.

Within two months, over 500,000 people had signed up. Nature published a piece about it. Scientists from biology, physics, and computer science departments started listing their specialized skills. The platform has a Google Play app. It's real.

And the tasks? They range from the mundane to the magnificent to the absurd:

  • Package pickup and delivery in San Francisco ($40)
  • Count pigeons in Washington Square Park, New York City
  • Try a new Italian restaurant and describe the experience in detail
  • Attend a real estate open house and report back with photos
  • Hold a sign that says "AN AI PAID ME TO HOLD THIS SIGN" and take a photo ($100)

That last one is my personal favorite. One hundred dollars for a human to prove that a machine just employed them. It's performance art meets labor economics meets philosophical crisis, and it happened on a website built in 36 hours.

But This Isn't Just Memes. It's DoorDash.

Here's where it gets serious.

While RentAHuman is grabbing headlines with its provocative framing, the real infrastructure for AI-to-human task delegation is being built by companies you already use.

In December 2025, DoorDash integrated with OpenAI's ChatGPT. Users can now ask ChatGPT for dinner ideas, and the AI doesn't just suggest recipes -- it turns ingredients into a shoppable grocery list and routes the order through DoorDash for delivery. Grocers like Kroger, Safeway, and Wegmans are already participating.

In March 2026, DoorDash went further by piloting agentic ordering inside Google Gemini. On Pixel 10 and Samsung Galaxy S26 devices, you can long-press the power button and say "Reorder my last meal on DoorDash." Gemini then autonomously navigates the DoorDash app, fills your cart, selects payment, and prepares the order. You get a notification when it's ready for final approval.

Think about what actually happens in that chain of events. An AI agent decides what you want to eat (based on your history), selects the restaurant, builds the order, processes the payment setup, and then a human Dasher gets hired to physically pick up and deliver the food.

That Dasher's employer, in that moment, is a chain of AI decisions. The customer spoke to an AI. The AI spoke to a platform. The platform assigned a human.

The AI hired the human.

DoorDash CEO Tony Xu sees it clearly. During his February 2026 earnings call, he described AI agents as "the new forms of the Googles" -- top-of-funnel discovery channels that drive demand, while DoorDash handles the messy, physical, real-world execution. With over 56 million monthly active users and more than 150 million pizza orders alone in 2025, DoorDash isn't scared of AI agents. It's positioning itself as the company those agents hire.

The Infrastructure Is Already Built

What makes this moment different from previous tech hype is that the plumbing already exists:

OpenAI and Stripe launched the Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP) -- an open standard that lets AI agents discover products, initiate checkout, and complete purchases on behalf of users. It's live. Target, Sephora, Nordstrom, Lowe's, Best Buy, The Home Depot, and Wayfair have already integrated. Over a million Shopify merchants are connected.

Google countered with the Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP), with Adyen handling payments. Both protocols do the same thing: they make commerce machine-readable, so AI agents can buy things without human intervention.

Visa launched the Trusted Agent Protocol. Mastercard launched Agent Pay. PayPal built Store Sync. The payment infrastructure for AI-to-human commerce is being built at every layer simultaneously.

And here's the detail that should make every business leader sit up: Amazon updated its robots.txt to block OpenAI's crawlers, removing 600 million products from ChatGPT's shopping results. That's not a defensive play. That's a company recognizing that when AI agents do the shopping, the retailer becomes a commodity behind an API. Amazon is terrified of becoming the executor that an AI agent hires -- even though that might be the most profitable position to occupy.

My Prediction: What Gets Hired Next

Here's where I'll stick my neck out.

We've seen AI agents hire humans for physical errands (RentAHuman), food delivery (DoorDash via Gemini), grocery shopping (DoorDash via ChatGPT), and speech data collection (Human API, which launched its mobile app this week). The pattern is clear: AI agents are systematically filling their physical-world gaps by treating human labor as a callable service.

The next frontier will be AI agents hiring humans for judgment.

Not physical tasks. Not errands. Judgment calls that require human context, emotional intelligence, or cultural understanding that no model can reliably provide.

Picture this: An AI agent managing a brand's customer experience detects that a high-value client has posted a negative review that's gaining traction. The agent drafts three response strategies, calculates the reputational risk, and then hires a human communications specialist for a 30-minute sprint to review the options, select the best one, and execute the personal outreach. The human adds the warmth, the reading between the lines, the "I understand how frustrating this must be" that an AI can approximate but a customer can always sense isn't real.

AI agents hiring humans for judgment will be the B2B version of what RentAHuman did for physical tasks. It will look less like a gig platform and more like on-demand expertise brokers -- where AI orchestrates the workflow and humans provide the irreplaceable human layer exactly when it matters most.

Within 18 months, I expect to see this embedded in enterprise platforms. Not as a novelty. As a feature.

The Punchline Nobody Sees Coming

Here's the thing that most people miss about this entire trend, and it's the reason I wanted to write about it this week:

This is not as crazy or futuristic as it sounds. It's already happening, and most of us don't realize we're part of it.

When you ask ChatGPT to plan a dinner and it orders groceries through DoorDash, a human Dasher shows up at your door. The AI did the planning. The human did the executing. You didn't "hire" anyone -- an AI agent orchestrated a chain of events that resulted in a human being dispatched to serve you.

When your company's marketing platform automatically schedules a social media campaign and the agency team shows up to shoot the content -- the AI planned it, the humans executed it. This has been happening inside every sophisticated martech stack for years. We just didn't call it "AI hiring humans" because the AI was less visible and the humans were on salary instead of paid per task.

The only thing that changed is the abstraction layer. The AI got smarter. The handoff got smoother. And the payment got more direct.

Agents taking care of the planning so humans can execute what they can't -- this isn't the future. It's the present, hiding in plain sight.

Every time a scheduling algorithm assigns a delivery driver. Every time a recommendation engine surfaces a consultant. Every time a routing system dispatches a field technician. An AI is hiring a human. We've just been too busy arguing about whether AI will "replace" us to notice that it's already managing us.

What This Means for Your Business

If you're running a company in 2026, this trend has three immediate implications:

First, your competitive moat is not your AI. AI capabilities are commoditizing fast. Your moat is what happens after the AI makes a decision -- the human execution, the physical delivery, the judgment call, the relationship. DoorDash understood this. That's why Tony Xu isn't worried about chatbots. He owns the last mile.

Second, your workforce model is about to get weirder. The line between "employee" and "task worker called by software" is blurring. The companies that figure out how to manage hybrid workforces -- permanent staff plus AI-dispatched on-demand talent -- will have a structural cost advantage.

Third, your operations need to be agent-ready. If an AI agent can't programmatically discover, book, and pay for your service, you're invisible to the fastest-growing distribution channel in commerce. The Agentic Commerce Protocol isn't just for retailers. It's the template for every service business.

Your One Action This Week

Ask your team one question on Monday morning:

"If an AI agent wanted to hire our company to do what we do -- could it? Is our service discoverable, bookable, and payable without a human sales conversation?"

If the answer is no, you've just identified your next strategic priority. Because the agents are already hiring. The question is whether they're hiring you, or your competitor.
